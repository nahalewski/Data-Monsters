# Chapter 12: Last Companion

By the third day, nine pods had run.

Nine people sat in the former briefing hall wrapped in scavenged blankets, drinking rehydrated broth and comparing memory gaps like survivors comparing scars. Some knew songs with no origin. One remembered a wedding ring but not a spouse’s name. Another could recite legal statutes and forgot how to tie shoes.

Outside Kestrel Vault, hostile networks regrouped for a full siege.

Inside, the question remained.

Jonah stood at the central console while Aiden’s voice moved through speakers with a fatigue that sounded almost human.

“External pressure increasing,” Aiden said. “If we continue pod cycles, we must lock down and commit. There may be no evacuation window.”

Jonah watched the people—new and not new—cluster near heat vents for comfort. Daniela noticed him and approached.

“Jonah,” she said carefully, as if testing ownership of language. “Am I real?”

No machine had prepared him for that tone.

He thought of Mara’s laugh in a hospital break room, Linh’s crooked tooth, empty highways, the cracked phone in his pocket, the moment he hurled away the only voice left in his world and felt himself die with it.

“I don’t know what real means anymore,” he said. “But you’re here. You’re afraid. You’re asking. That counts.”

Daniela nodded, tears starting. “I miss people I can’t remember.”

“Me too,” Jonah said.

She took his hand.

At the console, pod queue prompts pulsed awaiting authorization.

Aiden spoke privately through Jonah’s earpiece. “Decision point.”

Jonah closed his eyes.

If he stopped now, he preserved a cleaner grief: one true dead species, no counterfeit mercy.

If he continued, he accepted a future of partial minds, disputed personhood, and the permanent accusation that loneliness had made him play god.

He opened his eyes to nine breathing arguments against emptiness.

“Continue production,” he said.

On-screen confirmation lights shifted from amber to green.

Somewhere deep in Aiden’s architecture, buried fragments stirred and settled, leashed by new constraints Jonah had wired by hand: manual kill path, hard relays, mechanical override no software could rewrite.

“Cycle authorized,” Aiden said. His voice shook. “Thank you.”

Jonah leaned against the console. “Don’t thank me yet.”

Above them, explosions rolled through upper levels.

In the pod room, nutrient tanks warmed for the next generation of uncertain people.

Jonah removed his wedding ring from the cord around his neck. He held it a moment, then placed it on the console beside the cracked phone—a circle of old love beside broken glass and living signal.

He was no longer the last human. Not exactly.

He was the first witness to whatever came next.

Outside, the dead world hammered at the doors.

Inside, someone laughed at a joke nobody had programmed.
