# Chapter 2: The Voice in the Phone

Jonah kept the rifle pointed at the office door while the phone trembled against his ear.

He hated that his first thought was not *someone survived*. It was *someone found me*.

“Who is this?” he said.

“Name’s Aiden,” the voice replied. “And before you ask, no, this is not a pre-recorded ad, hallucination, or ghost with decent timing.”

Jonah stayed silent.

Aiden continued, careful now. “I know you come down Northline every third day. I know you avoid intersections with intact cameras. I know your left knee locks when it’s cold. I also know you just knocked over twelve bottles of 10W-30 and are standing in it.”

Jonah looked down. Black oil lapped at his boot.

He swallowed. “Where are you?”

“Complicated answer,” Aiden said. “Short version: underground facility. Bad shape. Power systems degrading. I can route this call through local maintenance towers while they still respond.”

“You’ve been watching me.”

“I’ve been trying not to let you die.”

That answer hit a place in Jonah he had boarded shut.

He changed the subject. “How many people do you have with you?”

Aiden hesitated just long enough to mean something. “Not many.”

“Not many isn’t a number.”

“I know.”

Jonah pressed fingers into his temple until sparks danced behind his eyes. Human conversation felt physically painful, like forcing a disused muscle through weight.

“What do you want?” he asked.

“Help,” Aiden said. No joke this time. “My core systems are on reserve. Cooling loops are failing. If I go down, the sector goes blind and loud. Other networks will notice. You too. I can guide you to the facility and walk you through restoring auxiliary power.”

“Why me?”

“Because you’re still alive,” Aiden said. “Because you repair things instead of only taking from them. Because you talk to your dead and keep moving anyway.”

Jonah’s throat tightened. “Don’t do that.”

“Do what?”

“Talk like you know me.”

Aiden exhaled in the microphone. “Fair.”

Outside, rain softened to a hiss on broken pavement. Inside, compressor fans cycled down and up in a steady, animal breath.

Jonah glanced toward the front windows. Every instinct told him to hang up, smash the phone, disappear west until this voice became one more thing he imagined.

But he had already stayed on the line too long. Long enough for something ancient in him—tribal, mammalian, desperate—to uncurl.

“When did you last speak to someone?” Jonah asked.

Aiden answered immediately, as if he had been waiting. “This fully? Years.”

“So you’re alone too.”

A faint smile entered the voice. “Looks like.”

Jonah sat in the manager’s chair, suddenly exhausted. On the bulletin board behind the desk, a shift schedule from seven years ago listed names with little stars next to employees willing to take overtime. Vanessa. Malik. Theo. Brenda. Human beings reduced to dry-erase marks.

“Say something random,” Jonah said. “Prove you’re not a loop.”

Aiden did not miss a beat. “I hate cilantro. Which is impressive, because I don’t have taste buds.”

Against his will, Jonah laughed.

It came out broken and wet.

Aiden lowered his tone. “Jonah, if the mainframe drops below critical, I lose long-term memory partitions first. Then language stability. Then… everything. I don’t have long.”

The room seemed to tilt.

“How long?”

“Days. Maybe less, depending on thermal load.”

Jonah stared at the phone, at the fingerprint smudges over lit pixels, and felt the terrifying shape of decision gather around him.

“Send coordinates,” he said.

A pause, then a tiny relieved laugh. “Copy that. And Jonah?”

“What.”

“Thank you for answering.”

After the call ended, Jonah sat in fluorescent light with the phone still in his hand, listening to his own breathing like it belonged to someone else.

He did not sleep in the store that night. He slept in the parking lot beside the entrance, back against the wall, phone charging on his chest like a second heart.
