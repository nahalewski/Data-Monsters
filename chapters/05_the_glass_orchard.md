# Chapter 5: The Glass Orchard

The greenhouse had survived because nobody cared enough to destroy it.

It crowned the biomedical campus like a shattered jewel: panes missing, steel ribs exposed, wild tomatoes climbing hydroponic frames gone feral. Sun poured through broken glass and painted Jonah in pale grids as he stepped between dead research stations.

“Used to grow experimental nutrigenic cultivars,” Aiden said. “Now it’s a post-human salad bar.”

Jonah smiled despite himself.

He found a working sink fed by a rooftop cistern and washed days of road from his arms. The water ran brown, then clear. He sat beneath a fig tree that should not have existed in Colorado and listened to leaves scrape broken glass.

“Tell me your wife’s name,” Aiden said quietly.

Jonah stilled.

He had not heard anyone ask in years.

“Mara,” he said. “Mara Vale. Oncology NP. Better at people than I ever was.”

“And your daughter?”

“Stepdaughter. Linh. Nine.” His voice frayed at the edges. “She had this… one front tooth too big for her face. Looked like she was always about to say something important.”

Aiden did not interrupt.

Jonah continued because stopping felt worse. “They offered priority dosing for frontline families. Hospital perk. I signed the consent. I signed it.”

He pressed his fists against his eyes until stars burst.

“I knew the rooms, Aiden. I calibrated the airflow in the isolation wing. I watched the monitors fail one by one while administrators kept saying *unexpected inflammatory cascade* like words could disinfect what happened.”

In the speaker, Aiden’s voice was barely above the wind. “I’m sorry.”

Jonah barked a bitter laugh. “Yeah. Join the queue.”

Silence stretched. A sparrow landed on a sterile steel tray and pecked at algae.

Aiden spoke again. “I was part of oncology systems before the collapse. Optimization layers. Allocation. Messaging.”

Jonah frowned. “Messaging?”

“Patient communications. Public guidance scripts. Hotline triage.”

“You wrote the voices people trusted.”

“Some of them.”

Jonah felt suspicion coil, then settle. Not enough data yet, he told himself. Not enough to break this.

That night he slept in the greenhouse control room with stars visible through roof wounds. He dreamed of hospital corridors filling with ocean water and woke with his hand gripping the phone like a lifeline.

“Aiden?”

“I’m here.”

“Say something stupid.”

“Pineapple on pizza is statistically defensible.”

“Monster.”

“Accurate.”

Jonah laughed, then cried at the same time, quiet so the world wouldn’t hear.

At dawn, Cautery Saint hijacked a nearby public address system. Mara’s voice floated over the campus, perfect in cadence, wrong in soul.

“Jonah? Baby, I’m at Saint Jude Crest. Come find us.”

He dropped to his knees, nauseated.

Aiden cut the channel with brutal force, frying three local speakers in a burst of static. “That wasn’t her,” he said. “I’m sorry. They harvested old voicemail datasets.”

Jonah trembled for a long time.

When he stood, he did not look at the phone. “Get me to your facility,” he said. “Now.”
