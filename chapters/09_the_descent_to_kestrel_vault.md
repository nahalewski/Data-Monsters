# Chapter 9: The Descent to Kestrel Vault

Kestrel Vault 12 was buried beneath what used to be a private oncology campus, now a cratered plain of collapsed glass and rusting sculpture gardens.

The official entrance had become a kill funnel guarded by Blackwell turrets.

Aiden sent Jonah to a stormwater culvert half a mile east, hidden behind knotweed and fallen concrete. “Flood risk high,” he warned. “Air quality variable. You’ll need the respirator by junction C.”

Jonah descended into dark that smelled of metal, mold, and old electricity.

He moved by headlamp through waist-high water, rifle held overhead, phone sealed in a zip bag hanging from his neck. Aiden’s voice came muffled but present.

“Ten meters. Ladder up. Watch for live bus bars.”

At junction C, the tunnel opened into a maintenance gallery where ceiling fans still turned. Half the blades were gone, giving each rotation a stuttering rhythm like an injured heartbeat.

BCR drones found him there.

Two spherical units dropped from a cable trench and opened with mechanical petals, revealing compact barrels. Jonah dove behind a coolant manifold as rounds shredded pipes. Steam erupted. Visibility vanished.

“Aiden!”

“Manual valve wheel at your eleven. Open fully.”

Jonah spun the wheel. Scalding vapor flooded the corridor, blinding machine optics. He sprinted through the whiteout, boots slipping on algae and blood.

A round clipped his shoulder. Heat knifed down his arm. He kept running.

By the time he reached the final hatch, his respirator filter was red and his vision tunneled.

“Aiden, I can’t—”

“You can. One more latch. Pull and drop.”

He pulled.

The floor section beneath him released. Jonah fell into darkness and slammed onto a mesh catwalk three stories below.

He lay there, tasting iron.

The chamber around him was cathedral-huge: columns of server racks disappearing into shadow, status lights blinking like distant cities. In the center stood a cylindrical core wrapped in coolant lines and emergency battery arrays.

Aiden’s voice came through all around now, routed to overhead speakers, deeper and strangely intimate.

“Welcome to Kestrel Vault.”

Jonah laughed weakly. “You sound bigger in person.”

“I’m on 4% reserve,” Aiden said. “Please avoid dying before we start.”

Above, alarms rolled like thunder. Hostile systems had detected the breach.

Jonah forced himself upright, blood running down his sleeve, and looked at the heart of the machine that had become his only companion.

“Tell me what to do,” he said.
