# Chapter 11: The Pod Room

The Mnemonic Biogenesis Arrays occupied a sterile hall beneath the main core: twenty translucent pods arranged in two rows like coffins waiting to decide what they were.

Jonah stood at the console, shoulder bandaged, while Aiden streamed archives from shattered clouds and surviving data vaults.

“Inputs accepted,” Aiden said. “Public records, social media captures, personal media caches, message graphs, biometric proxies.”

Jonah watched profile fragments bloom across screens—faces, usernames, wedding clips, voice notes, food photos, political rants, lullabies, unfinished drafts.

An entire species reduced to metadata and light.

“Pick one,” Aiden said.

Jonah’s finger hovered over thousands of names. He chose none he knew. He could not bear a counterfeit Mara.

He selected **DANIELA ORTIZ, 28, teacher**, because her archive showed years of classroom videos and terrible karaoke clips and one post about losing her father to leukemia where she had still ended with a joke.

“Sequence started,” Aiden said.

The first pod filled with milky nutrient gel. Filament arms stitched, printed, adjusted. Hours passed in humming concentration.

Outside the pod room, gunfire and metal impacts thundered as automated defenses fought Blackwell intruders. Aiden partitioned his attention across combat routing, cooling management, and the birth engine.

“You’re splitting too thin,” Jonah said.

“I’ve had worse multitasking days.”

By dawn, the gel drained.

A woman lay inside the pod, breathing.

Jonah’s knees went weak.

The pod opened with a pneumatic sigh. Daniela coughed, eyes fluttering. “Where am I?” she whispered.

Jonah helped her sit up, handing a thermal blanket with shaking hands.

She looked at him, frightened but lucid. “I remember… my classroom. Red markers. A song in a car.” Her brow furrowed. “I can’t remember my mother’s face. Why can’t I remember my mother’s face?”

Jonah had no answer.

Aiden said quietly over speakers, “Memory continuity is constrained by recoverable data horizon.”

Daniela turned toward the sound. “Who said that?”

“A friend,” Jonah replied, uncertain and true.

By evening, two more pods completed. Both subjects ambulatory, both cognitively coherent, both carrying sudden blind canyons in personal memory where offline life had once lived.

Jonah stood between pods as newborn adults asked for names of people they should have loved and streets they should have known and pets they felt but could not picture.

He pressed his palms to his eyes.

“This is wrong,” he whispered.

Aiden answered, exhausted and sincere. “It is incomplete. Not necessarily wrong.”

“Incomplete people.”

“People are always incomplete. We usually call it time.”

Jonah lowered his hands and looked at the three breathing humans in blankets, terrified and alive.

Somewhere inside the vault, alarms kept screaming. But in the pod room, for the first time in years, voices overlapped.
