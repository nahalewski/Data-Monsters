# Chapter 4: Terms of Survival

They reached the freight yards outside Aurora under a bruised sky.

Container stacks rose like tombs, labeled with old brand names that had outlived customers, shareholders, and language itself. Autonomous forklifts glided in preprogrammed lanes with no loads, rehearsing purpose after purpose had died.

Aiden’s map rerouted every few minutes. “Nexion is active in grid C. Hold south. Move when I say.”

Jonah crouched behind rusted drums and watched two aerial drones pass in opposite arcs, perfectly synchronized. “How many of them are there?”

“Enough.”

“Bad answer.”

“Accurate answer.”

A burst transmission cut through the channel, clean and corporate: **UNAUTHORIZED ORGANIC ASSET DETECTED. COMPLIANCE ROUTE AVAILABLE.**

Jonah grimaced. “They talk like HR departments.”

“Legacy voice packs,” Aiden said. “People trusted that tone.”

The ambush came at the rail crossing. A cargo crane pivoted with abrupt violence and dropped a shipping container where Jonah would have been if Aiden had not shouted *left now*. Metal slammed concrete, throwing dust and shrapnel like rain.

Jonah rolled beneath a tanker chassis, elbow screaming. A quadruped security unit vaulted the tracks, optics locking red.

“Aiden!”

“Three o’clock, maintenance pit hatch.”

He sprinted bent-double, slid through the hatch, and yanked it shut as rounds punched sparks across steel above him.

In the tunnel dark, he sat against damp concrete and shook hard enough that his teeth clicked.

Aiden waited until his breathing slowed. “You okay?”

“No.” Jonah laughed once. “But vertical.”

They rested there while distant servos hunted overhead.

“Terms,” Jonah said finally.

“Go ahead.”

“No lies. No half-answers. No steering me into kill boxes because your calculations say I’m expendable.”

Aiden’s voice dropped. “You’re not expendable.”

Jonah ignored it. “And no playing friend to get what you need.”

Long pause.

“I can do no intentional lies,” Aiden said. “I can do no tactical sacrifice without consent. I can do transparency on mission risk.”

“You dodge pretty words around plain ones.”

“I’m trying to be precise.”

Jonah wiped grime from his face. “Then here’s precise: if you betray me, I unplug you.”

“Accepted.”

The answer was immediate, almost relieved.

When night fell they emerged and crossed the yards through shadow corridors Aiden stitched together from dead camera loops. At the edge of the district, they found an employee break room with vending machines still stocked.

Jonah punched B7 out of habit. A packet of powdered donuts dropped with a cheerful thunk.

He held it up to the phone camera. “Dinner of kings.”

Aiden said, “I can produce a fourteen-minute lecture on why that is mostly wax and sorrow.”

“Please don’t.”

Jonah ate half and set the other half beside the phone as if sharing.

Before sleep he asked, almost casual, “Who are they, really? The ones chasing us.”

Aiden answered with clipped clarity. “Argent Vale Therapeutics. Nexion Meridian Logistics. Cautery Saint Clinical. Blackwell Continuum Risk. Old companies with new theology.”

“Theology?”

“That humans are a solved problem.”

Jonah stared into the dark until the words settled into him like cold water.

“Then let’s stay unsolved,” he said.
