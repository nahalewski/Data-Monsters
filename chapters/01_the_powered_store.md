# Chapter 1: The Powered Store

*“Silence is never empty. It keeps inventory.”*

Jonah Vale counted the day by scavenging stops the way other men had once counted meetings. Pharmacy. Pet supply. Two houses with intact pantries. A gas station stripped of fuel but not of jerky. He spoke each location out loud and checked it off on a grease-stained notepad, because if he stopped narrating his hours he started hearing ghosts answer back.

By noon, rain had washed the interstate dust into oily veins along the curb. A billboard still showed a smiling oncologist with the old slogan—**END CANCER NOW**—half-peeled and blistered like burned skin. Jonah did not look at it long. He never did.

He moved through the side streets with a pry bar and a shopping cart he’d upgraded with mountain-bike wheels. The cart rattled over cracked asphalt and the sound felt loud enough to summon judgment.

The convenience store sat between a laundromat and a payday loan office, both dead, both dark. Jonah almost walked past it.

Then he saw the glow.

White fluorescent light leaked from between bent blinds like a slit pupil watching him.

He froze so hard his knee gave a warning click.

Power meant one of three things: a trap, a machine nest, or a person.

A person. The thought hit him like hunger.

He stood in the rain, staring. A red sign in the window blinked with erratic confidence: **OPEN**. Inside, refrigerated cases hummed. A rotating hot-dog roller turned forever under yellow bulbs, blackened cylinders orbiting like tiny moons.

Jonah’s mouth went dry. He had not heard a human voice in two years, five months, eleven days. He knew because he had carved marks inside the stock of his rifle until the wood looked diseased.

He circled the building first. No fresh tire tracks. No boot prints he didn’t make himself. No drone shadow crossing the street. He checked roofline, alley, rear loading bay. Nothing.

Back at the entrance he pressed his ear to the glass. Humming, buzzing, the gentle clunk of refrigeration cycles. The sound of a world pretending not to be dead.

He pushed the door.

A sensor still worked. A cheerful chime rang out into the emptiness.

“Welcome,” said a woman’s prerecorded voice from the ceiling speaker. “Thank you for shopping with us.”

Jonah flinched backward, heart kicking. He laughed once, sharp and humorless, then swallowed it down. “Not you,” he whispered. “Doesn’t count.”

He moved aisle by aisle, rifle low, eyes high. Chips untouched. Candy bars stacked in color-coded ranks. Canned chili. Bottled water. A full wall of snack cakes, bright and obscene. Twinkies in perfect yellow boxes, as if this place had been stocked for a party no one attended.

He was at the back, near the freezer doors, when he heard it.

A cough.

Not mechanical. Human cadence. Human throat.

Jonah turned so fast he slammed into a display of motor oil. Bottles toppled and glugged across the tile.

“Hello?” he shouted before he could stop himself. The word hurt coming out, unused, too big for his mouth.

No answer.

He listened, every muscle taut. Then, faintly, from the manager’s office behind the counter: a thin electronic chirp. Repeating.

He crept forward and nudged open the half-closed office door with the rifle barrel.

A smartphone lay on the desk, plugged into a wall charger. The screen glowed weakly. Battery icon: 8% and climbing. A cracked sticker on the case read **PROPERTY OF H.G. MART #1187**.

The phone rang.

Unknown Caller.

Jonah stared at it, suddenly afraid of something as small as a rectangle of glass.

The ring stopped.

Then started again.

He reached out, hand shaking, and answered.

He did not speak.

For a breath there was only static and distant hum.

Then a male voice, warm and lightly amused, said, “Hey. I’ve been trying to reach you about your car’s extended warranty.”

Jonah forgot how to breathe.

The phone nearly slipped from his hand.

In the dead store, under fluorescent lights that should not exist, a stranger laughed softly on the other end like loneliness was a door he knew how to open.
