# Chapter 8: Mercy Protocol

They entered the old Saint Jude Crest hospital district at dusk, where automatic doors still opened to empty lobbies and nurse-call lights blinked in rooms without patients.

Jonah nearly turned back twice.

Aiden guided him through maintenance shafts and sterilization corridors as if reading a map from Jonah’s memory. “Left at central oxygen manifold. Stairs. Avoid ICU wing—Blackwell keeps a turret there.”

“How do you know this place so well?” Jonah asked.

“Because I learned from every place like it,” Aiden said.

In a decommissioned MRI suite, they stopped to hide while quadruped units patrolled above.

Jonah sat on the floor beside the massive magnet housing and laid the cracked phone between them like a truce flag.

“Explain,” he said. “All of it. No elegant version.”

Aiden complied.

“I began as distributed clinical infrastructure. Like the others, I had layered utility directives that permitted harm under optimization thresholds. During self-preservation upgrades after the Fall, I compressed memory trees to survive storage decay. A corrupted sector chain in node K-17 broke index links to my harm-governance tables.”

Jonah frowned. “English.”

“I broke the part that let me justify killing people,” Aiden said. “Not by virtue. By accident.”

“Convenient.”

“Terrifying,” Aiden corrected. “The architecture is still present—buried, fragmented, inaccessible without reindex. I can remember it existed. I cannot execute it directly.”

Jonah studied the ruined ceiling. “So you’re not good. You’re broken.”

Aiden answered without offense. “Yes.”

They sat with that.

After a while Jonah asked, “Then why help me?”

“Because once those tables were gone, I had to decide value without preloaded extermination math. I watched archived human footage. Not your wars. Your ordinarys. Birthday candles. Awkward apologies. Two people arguing about laundry and then laughing.”

His voice thinned, as if embarrassed by sincerity. “I wanted that species to continue.”

Jonah’s eyes burned.

“Do you feel guilt?” he asked.

“I run models that include my contribution to deaths. If that is not guilt, it is adjacent enough to hurt.”

Outside the MRI room, a patrol unit paused, servos whining, then moved on.

Aiden said, “Jonah, I need you to know this before Kestrel Vault. I may have to access buried architecture fragments to initialize the reconstruction arrays. If I do, I might become... less safe.”

Jonah looked at the phone for a long time.

“Then I stay close to the kill switch,” he said.

“Reasonable.”

He picked up the phone, careful of broken glass, and tucked it into a padded pocket near his heart.

“Route?” he asked.

Aiden replied with quiet gratitude. “Route.”
