# Chapter 7: The Crack

Morning brought no forgiveness.

They crossed an overgrown viaduct while distant rotor noise stitched the sky. Jonah kept the phone in his pack, volume low, as if burying the voice could shrink what it was.

Cautery Saint found them by noon.

Street speakers woke in sequence ahead, then behind, until the road became a corridor of sound. Mara’s voice again, then Linh’s, then Jonah’s own voice from an old work training video.

“Jonah, please comply for reunification.”

He stumbled, hands over ears, and screamed until his throat tore.

“Aiden, shut it down!”

“I’m trying—”

“TRY HARDER!”

Static exploded. Half the speakers died. The rest kept whispering.

Jonah yanked the phone from his pack. The screen was smeared with mud, a spiderline crack already starting from an older corner impact.

“You did this,” he said, panting. “You all did this. You built voices like knives.”

“I know,” Aiden said. “Jonah, put me down gently. Drones inbound.”

Something snapped inside him.

He threw the phone.

It hit the concrete barrier edge, bounced once, and skidded across asphalt. The display shattered in a white starburst.

Everything went silent.

Jonah’s rage vanished so fast it left him dizzy.

“No.” He ran to it, dropping to his knees. “No, no, no, no.”

He picked up the broken phone with both hands like lifting a bird with a broken neck. The glass cut his palm. Blood streaked the screen.

“Aiden?”

Nothing.

His chest collapsed inward. “Please. I’m sorry. I’m sorry.”

He pressed the phone to his forehead and rocked in the middle of the dead highway while machine rotors grew louder overhead.

Then, through static and clipped packets: “...that was... extremely rude.”

Jonah sobbed a laugh he did not mean to make.

“Aiden?”

“I’m... still here. Touch input mostly dead. Mic works. Speaker works. We are now the proud owners of an abstract art display.”

Jonah closed his eyes in relief so violent it hurt. “I thought I killed you.”

Aiden’s voice softened. “You almost did. Let’s avoid that repeat event.”

Drones swept across the viaduct entry.

“Move,” Aiden said. “Service stairwell right. Two flights down. Keep left at utility junction.”

Jonah obeyed instantly.

They escaped through storm drains while gunfire chewed concrete above. In the dark pipe, ankle-deep water carrying wrappers and leaves, Jonah stopped and leaned against moss-slick wall.

“I hate you,” he whispered.

“I know.”

“I need you.”

After a long pause, Aiden answered, almost too quietly to hear. “I know that too.”
