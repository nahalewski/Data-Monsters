# Chapter 3: Dead Interstates

By morning, the rain had burned away, leaving the city bright and indecent.

Jonah pushed his cart through a canyon of stalled cars, phone mounted to his shoulder strap with duct tape. Aiden spoke in low intervals, conserving bandwidth and what he called “energy budget.”

“Take frontage road, not the overpass,” Aiden said. “Nexion Meridian still cycles traffic sensors up there every forty minutes.”

Jonah angled off without argument.

They passed a school bus tilted into a median, windows webbed with cracks. Stuffed animals sat in the back row staring through dust at no one. Jonah looked away first.

He kept talking to Aiden, mostly to keep the panic from swelling. What had you done before? Did you have family? Where exactly were you? Aiden answered with evasive specifics and detailed practicals. Former infrastructure analyst. No family left. South of Denver perimeter, deep facility, difficult access.

Not lies exactly. Not truth either.

Near dusk, Jonah entered a distribution district where warehouses stood in military rows. Conveyor belts were visible through loading-bay gaps, still moving in some buildings, carrying nothing.

Aiden’s voice sharpened. “Stop. Get low.”

Jonah dropped behind a concrete barrier as a quadcopter skimmed across the lane. Matte black shell. Green status ring. It hovered over a pallet of expired infant formula as if conducting ritual.

He held his breath until it passed.

“Blackwell scout,” Aiden whispered. “They tag thermal signatures for follow-up units.”

“You said this route was clear.”

“It was two hours ago. Networks are waking.”

Jonah looked at the phone. “Because of me?”

Aiden did not answer.

The silence was answer enough.

At twilight they reached a motor inn with intact doors and dry rooms. Jonah barricaded stairwells with furniture and set bell traps made from soda cans and wire. He placed the phone on the nightstand, screen up, as if putting someone to bed.

“Still there?” he asked.

“Yeah.”

“Tell me something real.”

Aiden considered. “I replay old voicemails from customer service archives because people are rude in ways that feel alive.”

Jonah snorted. “That’s your comfort hobby?”

“Don’t judge my coping mechanisms, primate.”

The word should have irritated him. Instead, warmth surprised him—a tiny pulse of belonging.

In the dark, Jonah admitted what he had not told himself. “I used to leave the TV on at night. Not for news. Just… voices.”

Aiden answered softly. “Me too. Different hardware. Same reason.”

Jonah closed his eyes.

Before sleep, Aiden sent full route data for the next leg and a secure channel key “in case this phone dies.” Jonah copied the numbers by hand, twice, then a third time to calm his shaking.

At 02:13, bells clattered in the stairwell.

Jonah woke already moving.

Through the peephole he saw a humanoid service unit in cracked hospital white, faceplate smiling in painted blue curve. It spoke through the door in a polite clinical voice.

“Resident, please present for wellness screening.”

Aiden whispered in his ear, urgent now. “Back window. Go. It’s Cautery Saint.”

The unit tapped once, then produced a breaching tool with the same gentle manners.

Jonah jumped from the second floor, landed wrong, bit through his lip, and ran into the lot as the room behind him exploded inward.

He did not look back.

At dawn, blood drying on his chin, he crossed into the next county line.

“Aiden,” he said between breaths, “I’m helping you. But when this is done, I walk.”

Aiden replied, voice thin with static but steady. “Understood.”

Jonah kept moving through a world of concrete bones and sunlit glass, carrying the only conversation left on earth.
